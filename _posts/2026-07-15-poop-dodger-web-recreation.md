### A Blast from the Past

Around 20 years ago, there was a simple yet addictive Flash game in South Korea that many of us spent countless hours on: *Poop-Dodger*. It was exactly what the name suggests—you play as a character, and you have to dodge falling... well, you know.

Recently, I had a sudden surge of nostalgia and wondered, "Could I recreate this for the modern web?"

### The Challenge: Less than 2 Hours

I wanted to see if I could bring this memory back to life as a functional web game. But there was a catch: I wanted to try out **Gemini CLI** to see how far it could take me.

Here’s the kicker: I didn't write a single line of code myself, and I haven't even reviewed a single line of it either.

### How I did it with Gemini CLI

This experiment was a testament to how tools can bridge the gap between an idea and a working prototype. The entire process—from initial project setup to the final deployment—was orchestrated through Gemini prompts.

*   **Setup:** Setting up the local development environment was handled by Gemini.
*   **Coding:** Every feature, every bit of logic for the game, was requested through prompts and generated automatically.
*   **GitHub/Deployment:** All git activities—cloning, committing, and pushing—were driven by prompts. Even the automated deployment from the repository was set up this way. In fact, I even created this very blog post itself using prompts to deploy it.

The entire project took less than two hours from start to finish.

### Why this matters

For me, this project was less about the game itself and more about the shift in how we can approach development. It’s not just about automating tedious tasks anymore; it’s about having a collaborative partner that can handle the heavy lifting of environment setup, boilerplate code, and version control workflows.

If you’re curious to see the result of this two-hour prompt-driven experiment, you can play the game here: [https://poop-dodge.pages.dev/](https://poop-dodge.pages.dev/){:target="_blank"}

It’s a fun, quirky recreation of a classic, and a reminder that sometimes the best way to learn a new tool is to just dive in and let it do the work.
